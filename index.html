Water from
the Well

Give water to the thirsty traveler, warmth to the shivering stranger, and a friendly ear to those who seek it, for in showing kindness, we find connection.

There is an old idea, older than the stories that carried it to us, that the past does not disappear. It collects. It deepens. It becomes the water beneath everything living, the source that feeds the roots, that rises through the tree. The Norse called it Urðr’s Well. Everything accomplished, everything survived, everything witnessed accumulates there. The present grows from it whether we know it or not.

I spent six years on the streets. Deep in my addiction, walking through the night to stay warm, watching people move through their lives without looking up. I cycled through every way I could find to stop and couldn't. I watched others do the same. I saw what despair looks like when it becomes ordinary, to the person living it and to everyone passing by.

None of that went away.

Give water to
the thirsty traveler

There is a particular kind of thirst that has nothing to do with water. I know what it is to need something so completely that the need becomes the whole world, and to watch that need go unmet, day after day, while the rest of the world moves through its routines without a second look.

On the streets I was thirsty in every sense of the word. For warmth. For stability. For someone to see me standing there. Addiction has a way of narrowing the world to the next hour, the next fix, the next way to get through the night. You stop being a person with a history and a future. You become only the present moment of your need.

What I didn't understand then was that the need itself was going into the well. Every sleepless night, every moment of invisibility, every time someone stepped around me on the sidewalk, it was accumulating. Not disappearing. Becoming something.

The well doesn't ask what you put into it. It receives everything.

Warmth to the
shivering stranger

Cold is something you stop noticing after a while. Not because it goes away but because fighting it becomes so constant it just becomes the texture of existing. You walk because standing still means the cold wins. You learn which places will leave you alone and which ones won't.

What stays with you longer than the cold itself is the strangeness of being a stranger in a place you know completely. I knew every face in that community of people the city preferred not to see. I was not a stranger to that world. But to everyone moving through on their way to somewhere else, I was invisible. A problem that wasn't theirs. A life that didn't register.

Warmth rarely came from a building or a fire. It came from the people living alongside you in the same conditions. Someone who knew your name. Someone who didn't walk past. The smallest acknowledgment that you were present and human could carry you further than a meal. That kind of warmth costs nothing to give.

It too went into the well. The cold and the warmth both. The indifference and the moments of recognition. All of it accumulating, becoming the ground of what came after.

And a friendly ear
to those who seek it

Being heard is not the same as being seen. You can be looked at and still be invisible. But when someone actually listens, stops, stays, gives you their full attention without an agenda or a clock running, something shifts. You become real in a way that survival alone doesn't provide.

I watched people carry stories that had nowhere to go. The weight of a life that nobody would miss if it ended. That weight is its own kind of addiction. It fills the same hollow that substances fill. The need to matter to someone briefly, or even once.

I was not good at asking to be heard. I'm still not. What I learned instead was how to listen. Sitting with someone in the dark, not fixing anything, not offering a solution, just being present while they said the thing they had been carrying. That turned out to be one of the most significant things I know how to do. I learned it in conditions that stripped everything else away.

What you hear from others goes into the well too. Their stories become part of the water. Their survival becomes evidence you draw from when you need to believe that survival is possible.

For in showing kindness,
we find connection

This is the line that took the longest to understand. Not because it's complicated but because for a long time I didn't believe it. Kindness felt like something that happened to other people, in other lives. Openness was a vulnerability. Connection meant something that could be taken away.

What changed wasn't a single moment. It was accumulation. The well filling slowly, layer by layer, until the weight of everything survived became something that could be offered rather than just carried. The cold nights, the invisibility, the people I watched disappear into their need and the ones I watched find their way back out, all of it became a kind of knowledge that only moves in one direction.

When someone shares a piece of their story with me, I try to give them the thing I needed most when I was struggling: my attention. Not advice. Not answers. Just the willingness to sit, listen, and let them know they are not carrying it alone. Because I know what it meant when someone did that for me, and I know what it costs when nobody does.

This is what the well is. Not a metaphor. The actual mechanism by which what was survived becomes what sustains. Urðr’s water rises through the roots, feeds the living tree, falls back as dew, and finds its way home. Nothing is lost. Everything accomplished, everything endured, everything witnessed accumulates and feeds forward.

The past does not drain away. It becomes the water you give to the thirsty traveler. The warmth you offer the shivering stranger. The ear you lend to the one who needs to be heard.

It becomes the kindness that finds its way back to the well.

Cavin Krenik
